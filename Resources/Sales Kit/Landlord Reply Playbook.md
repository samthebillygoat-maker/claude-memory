# Landlord Reply Playbook

# Landlord Reply Playbook — CitySide Stays

A Voss-based response system for inbound replies from landlords on Craigslist / Zillow / Apartments.com outreach. Use this when a landlord responds to a cold email, voicemail, or SMS.

---

## Voss Principles Cheat Sheet

Tactical empathy is not agreement — it is demonstrating that you understand the other side's position so completely that they stop defending it. Landlords don't reply because they are sold; they reply because they want to be heard. Every response below leads with acknowledgment (mirror or label) before moving to information or ask. Never start with "I" — start with "It sounds like" or "It seems like." Use no-oriented questions ("Is now a bad time?", "Have you given up on getting top dollar?") because "no" gives the landlord a feeling of safety and control. Front-load every objection they have not yet voiced (accusation audit) so they cannot use it against you later. When negotiating rent, use the Ackerman sequence: 65 / 85 / 95 / 100, with non-round numbers and a non-monetary concession thrown in at the final step. Tone matters more than words — the late-night FM DJ voice (slow, low, deliberate) calms the nervous system and signals you are not desperate.

---

## DO / DON'T

**DON'T:**
- Don't say "I understand." It is the most distrusted phrase in negotiation. They will assume you don't.
- Don't say "to be honest with you" or "honestly" — implies everything else was a lie.
- Don't say "trust me."
- Don't lead with the rent number. Defer until they have qualified the property to you.
- Don't use the word "Airbnb" before the words "professional" or "fully-insured."
- Don't apologize for cold outreach. Confidence sells.
- Don't send a third follow-up after silence. Two is the cap on the same thread.
- Don't make round-number offers. $2,139 anchors harder than $2,100.

**DO:**
- Do say "It sounds like..." / "It seems like..." / "It looks like..."
- Do mirror their last 1-3 words as a question, then shut up.
- Do front-load their objections before they raise them.
- Do ask "How am I supposed to do that?" when they push hard on price.
- Do ask "What would need to be true for this to work for you?" to surface real objections.
- Do write short. Two sentence paragraphs. White space sells.
- Do sign off with a no-oriented question that invites opt-out.

## INTERESTED — landlord is curious, wants to know more

**Triggers:** "Tell me more," "How does this work?", "Sounds interesting," "What are the details?"

**Subtext:** They are leaning in but skeptical. They want to be reassured this is not a scam without you actually saying "this is not a scam."

**Response template:**
> Hey {{landlord_name}} — appreciate you coming back to me on {{address}}.
>
> Quick context so you can decide if this is worth a conversation: I run a small business that takes 12-month leases on properties like yours and uses them for furnished mid-term stays — corporate relocations, traveling nurses, insurance-displaced families. Every unit is fully insured, professionally cleaned between guests, and I am the named tenant on the lease.
>
> You are probably wondering who actually stays there, what happens if there is damage, and why I am not just renting traditionally. Happy to walk through all three on a 10-minute call.
>
> Is Wednesday or Thursday a bad time to connect?

---

## PRICE_PUSHBACK — they want more than my offer

**Triggers:** "I need {{their_offer}}," "That's below market," "I can get more on the open market."

**Subtext:** They have an anchor in their head. They want to feel they negotiated, not capitulated.

**Response template (Ackerman, assumes target = {{my_offer}}):**
> {{their_offer}}?
>
> It sounds like you have a number in mind based on what comparable units are pulling in {{city}}. That makes sense.
>
> Here is my problem — at {{their_offer}}, the math does not work for me to take a 12-month commitment with zero vacancy risk to you. How am I supposed to make that work?
>
> What I can do is start at 65% of my ceiling and stretch from there. My honest opening is [65% non-round]. I have flexibility to move toward [85% non-round] if we can lock 12 months with a 30-day out clause for you, and I would cover the first professional deep-clean on move-out as a throw-in at [95% non-round]. The absolute top of what the unit pencils for me is {{my_offer}}.
>
> Where does that land for you?

---

## SKEPTICAL — STR doesn't work here / liability concerns

**Triggers:** "Airbnb is illegal here," "My HOA won't allow it," "What about liability?", "I've heard horror stories."

**Subtext:** They have read one bad article or had one bad neighbor. They want you to prove you are not that guy.

**Response template:**
> Horror stories?
>
> It sounds like you have either been burned before or watched somebody close to you get burned. That is a completely fair place to be starting from.
>
> A few things you are probably thinking that I want to address head-on: that the city will shut it down, that your insurance won't cover it, and that the neighbors will revolt. I operate on mid-term stays (30+ nights), which sit under a different regulatory bucket than nightly Airbnb in most {{city}} jurisdictions. I carry a $2M commercial liability policy with the property owner named as additional insured. And I screen every guest with ID + employer verification.
>
> What would need to be true about the operator for you to be open to a conversation?

---

## ASKING_QUESTIONS — specific clarifying questions

**Triggers:** "Who actually stays there?", "What insurance do you carry?", "How do you handle damage?", "How often do guests turn over?"

**Subtext:** They are doing diligence. This is a buying signal disguised as a question. Answer crisply. Do not over-explain.

**Response template:**
> Good questions — let me take them in order.
>
> Guests: traveling nurses on 13-week contracts, corporate relocations, and insurance-displaced families. Average stay is 47 nights. No bachelor parties, no weekend rentals.
>
> Insurance: $2M commercial general liability, you are named as additional insured, and I carry a separate $50k contents policy on the furniture.
>
> Damage: I hold the security deposit you require on the lease. Beyond that, my own ops budget covers wear-and-tear so you are not getting nickel-and-dimed.
>
> You are probably also wondering what happens if I default. The 12-month lease means you are made whole regardless of my occupancy. Want to jump on a quick call to walk through the lease structure?

---

## NEEDS_CONTRACT — wants paperwork first

**Triggers:** "Send me the lease," "I want to see the contract," "What does the agreement look like?"

**Subtext:** They are serious but defensive. They want leverage before the call.

**Response template:**
> Makes sense — you want to see what you are signing before we burn time on a call.
>
> I will send the standard 12-month lease and the insurance COI by end of day. One ask before I do: the lease has a couple of clauses (subletting permission, furnishings ownership, early-termination by you) that read aggressively in writing but are standard for this structure. Rather than have you read them cold, would it be unreasonable to spend 10 minutes walking through them together?
>
> If after that you want to take it to your attorney, that is the right move.
>
> Does Thursday work, or is this week already shot?

---

## HARD_NO — explicit rejection

**Triggers:** "Not interested," "I don't do STR," "Take me off your list," "No subletting, period."

**Subtext:** They mean it. Do not push. But leave a door open — 30% of hard-nos reverse within 6 months when their tenant flakes.

**Response template:**
> Understood, {{landlord_name}}. Off the list.
>
> One last thing and then I am gone: it sounds like you have had subletting go sideways before, or you have a hard rule against it. Either is fair.
>
> If your situation on {{address}} ever changes — vacancy stretches, tenant breaks lease, market softens — my number is below. No follow-ups from me otherwise.
>
> Appreciate the straight answer.

---

## SOFT_NO — "let me think about it" / silence

**Triggers:** "Let me think," "I'll get back to you," "Send me info and I'll review," then nothing.

**Subtext:** They are avoiding conflict. The real objection is unspoken.

**Response template:**
> Hey {{landlord_name}} — wanted to circle back on {{address}}.
>
> It seems like the timing or the structure isn't quite right, and I would rather know that than chase you.
>
> What would need to be true for this to be a no?
>
> If the answer is "nothing, it is a no," that is a totally fine response and I will stop reaching out.

---

## COMPETITOR — already doing STR with someone else

**Triggers:** "I already have an arbitrage tenant," "I'm working with another operator," "My unit is already on Airbnb."

**Subtext:** They are validating that the model works. They are also probably underpaid by the current operator.

**Response template:**
> Already with an operator?
>
> Smart — it sounds like you have already done the homework on whether this model works in {{city}}, which means I can skip the 101.
>
> Two questions: are they paying you on time every month, and is the rent indexed to what the unit could pull today versus what it pulled 18 months ago?
>
> If both answers are yes, you are in good hands and I will leave you alone. If either is a maybe, worth a 10-minute call to compare. Bad time to chat this week?

---

## HOSTILE — angry about cold outreach

**Triggers:** "How did you get my number?", "Stop spamming me," "This is harassment," profanity.

**Subtext:** Somebody else's cold call ruined their day, and you are the one who picked up the receipt. Do not match energy. Lower it.

**Response template (use FM DJ voice if on phone):**
> {{landlord_name}}, you are right to be annoyed. You posted a listing to find a tenant, not to get pitched.
>
> I got your contact off the public {{address}} listing — same place a normal tenant would. That does not make the interruption okay.
>
> I will take you off my list today. If you ever want to talk on your terms, my number is below. Otherwise you will not hear from me again.

---

## COUNTER_OFFER — landlord proposes different terms

**Triggers:** "I'll do it for 6 months instead of 12," "Only if you pay 3 months upfront," "I want a personal guarantee."

**Subtext:** They are negotiating, which means they are in. The terms they propose tell you what they actually fear.

**Response template:**
> Six months instead of twelve?
>
> It sounds like you want to test this without locking in for a full year in case it goes sideways. That is a reasonable place to be.
>
> Here is my challenge — at 6 months, I cannot recover the furnishing and onboarding cost on {{address}}, which runs me roughly the equivalent of two months of rent before I take a single guest. How am I supposed to make that pencil?
>
> What I can offer: full 12-month lease with a 60-day out clause that you control after month 4. You get the optionality of a short lease, I get the runway to make the unit work. Does that get us closer?

## Follow-Up Sequences (for non-responders)

Use these only when there has been zero reply to the initial outreach. Stop sending after Day 30. No exceptions.

### Day 5 — Accusation Audit

> Subject: probably the wrong time
>
> Hey {{landlord_name}} —
>
> You are probably thinking this is another arbitrage pitch, that you have already heard this one before, and that 12-month commitments from strangers on the internet are too good to be true.
>
> All fair.
>
> The only thing I want to know is whether {{address}} is still available, or whether I should close the file. Two-word reply works.
>
> Sam

### Day 10 — Calibrated Question

> Subject: {{address}}
>
> {{landlord_name}} —
>
> It seems like the listing might already be filled, or the timing is off on your end. Either is fine.
>
> What would need to be true for a guaranteed 12-month tenant on {{address}} to be worth 10 minutes on the phone?
>
> If the answer is nothing, that is the answer. No hard feelings.
>
> Sam

### Day 30 — No-Oriented Close-Out

> Subject: closing the file on {{address}}
>
> {{landlord_name}} —
>
> Have you given up on finding a 12-month tenant for {{address}}, or is it still on the market?
>
> I am closing my file on this one today either way. If the situation changes in the next few months, my number is below — text me directly and you skip the queue.
>
> Appreciate the time, even if the answer was no.
>
> Sam
